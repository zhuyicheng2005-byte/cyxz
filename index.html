Challenge One

Product Sale

Take a generic Taobao item and transform it into a brand people remember.

From Commodity to Brand
— ANCHOR —
Act I · Product
04 / 18
The Starting Point

A Forgettable Phone Stand

A basic folding aluminum phone stand — a generic ¥15–20 item sold by dozens of unbranded Taobao sellers, usually photographed on a plain white background with no story attached.

The Challenge
How do you make people feel something about a product they scroll past forty times a day?
Before · Commodity
— · —
Act I · Rebrand
05 / 18
New Identity

ANCHOR

A still point for your scattered day.

Material
Clay Grey
Matte aluminum with warm terracotta accent ring
Design Language
Intentional
The logo is the 45° angle the stand makes when you set it down
Promise
62 grams
One hinge. Zero charging. Nothing asking for attention.
After · Brand
— · —
Act I · Strategy
06 / 18
The Insight

You're not addicted
to your phone

You're just never asked to put it down.

Most days, your phone moves from your hand to your pocket to your hand again — maybe forty times before lunch. Nowhere to set it down means nowhere to look away from it, either.

The reframe: from product feature to emotional truth
ANCHOR isn't about using your phone less. It's about putting it somewhere on purpose, instead of always in your hand.
Insight · Behavioral Truth

Challenge Two

Brand Rebirth

Take a cultural icon and make it speak to a generation that values authenticity over perfection.

From Icon to Individual
— BARBIE IRL —
Act II · Context
08 / 18
The Legacy

One Look.
One Standard.

For decades, Barbie meant one thing: a specific silhouette, a signature blonde, an idealized beauty standard. You could be a doctor Barbie or an astronaut Barbie — but you looked like Barbie.

The Gen Z Problem
A generation that celebrates being yourself doesn't want to be told there's one way to look iconic.
Before · One Beauty Standard
— · —
Act II · Rebrand
09 / 18
Sub-Brand Concept

Barbie IRL

Be You. For Real.

BRAND POSITIONING STATEMENT

For Gen Z who see identity as fluid and self-expression as non-negotiable, Barbie IRL is the sub-brand that celebrates realness over perfection — where every body type, aesthetic, and ambition gets to be iconic.

Cultural Fluency
Authenticity
Not aspirational fantasy — relatable identities Gen Z already embodies: streetwear creative, soft minimalist, sporty it-girl
Their Language
Self-Aware
Meme-literate, lowercase energy, personality-led storytelling — less polished, more "main character energy"
Core Values
Inclusivity
Diverse bodies, skin tones, gender expressions — representation that reflects their reality, not a marketing checklist
Identity · Respecting Legacy, Speaking Gen Z

Act II · Ad Copy
10 / 18
Instagram Carousel · 5 Slides

barbie grew up.

Lowercase, casual tone — speaking their language

slide 1
remember when barbie had one look? yeah, we don't either.
slide 2
different bodies. different aesthetics. different ways to be iconic.
slide 3
soft girl. streetwear creative. sporty minimalist. vintage maximalist. main character energy in every form.
slide 4
this is barbie irl. where "you can be anything" means you can be yourself.
slide 5
be you. for real.
WHY THIS WORKS

Lowercase = approachable. "Main character energy" = their meme language. Diverse aesthetics over professions = respects how Gen Z self-identifies. Authenticity isn't a claim — it's built into the structure.

Challenge Three

Social Impact

Write copy that doesn't just ask for attention — it earns action.

From Awareness to Action
— UNESCO —
Act III · Context
12 / 18
The Mission

133 Million Girls

Not in school. Not because they failed — because no one built them a way in.

UNESCO tracks who is missing from classrooms, funds the programs that bring them back, and pushes governments to close the gap between who is enrolled on paper and who is actually sitting at a desk. Through Global Education Monitoring and girls'-education initiatives, they work district by district to remove the barriers.

The copywriting challenge
That number — 133 million — is too large to picture. How do you make the problem feel real without overwhelming the reader into inaction?
Issue · Girls' Education Access
— · —
Act III · Strategy
13 / 18
The Approach

Make It Specific

Don't picture the number. Picture the size of the actual problem.

A school gate she can't reach. A fee her family can't stretch to cover. A road that isn't safe after dark. Every one of those barriers has already been taken down somewhere, by someone — a community that built a girls' toilet block, a stipend that paid for a uniform, a night class scheduled around a daughter's farm work.

One Term
¥210
Safe transport for one girl
One Year
¥850
Teacher training that keeps her in the classroom
Long-Term
¥3,500
Safe, private sanitation that keeps girls enrolled past puberty
Specificity · Concrete Barriers
— · —
Act III · Copy
14 / 18
The Headline

Somewhere right now, a girl is doing everything right — and still isn't in school.

Not because she failed a test. Because no one built her a way in.

Hook · Emotional Resonance
— · —
Act III · Body
15 / 18
Core Message

Your donation doesn't
buy an education

It buys the bus fare, the toilet block, the night-class teacher's salary — the unglamorous, specific things that get her through the gate.

UNESCO doesn't fix this from one office with one grant. It works district by district: training teachers, pressing governments to make secondary school genuinely free, and building the data that shows which interventions actually move a girl from "enrolled on paper" to "sitting in the classroom."

The Call to Action
Open the gate for one more girl. Donate today.
133 million mornings are waiting to look different. Yours can help start that.
Clarity · Tangible Impact
